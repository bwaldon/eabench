Yes—possibly, but only under FERPA’s limited exceptions, and you should be careful about what you disclose.

### Short answer
FERPA generally prohibits disclosing education records without parent consent, **unless an exception applies**. In your scenario, the most relevant exception is the **health or safety emergency exception** in **20 U.S.C. § 1232g(b)(1)(I)**, which allows disclosure to **appropriate persons** if the information is **necessary to protect the health or safety of the student or others**.

Given a student essay with **graphic descriptions of harming other students**, disclosure to **local law enforcement** may be permitted **without parental consent** if the school reasonably determines there is an **articulable and significant threat**.

### What FERPA permits
Under the emergency exception, the school may disclose education records to people such as:
- law enforcement,
- school safety personnel,
- mental health crisis responders,
- other school officials,

**if knowledge of the information is necessary to protect health or safety**.

### Important limits
You should not assume this means “send everything.” FERPA supports disclosure of information that is **necessary** for the safety purpose.

That means:

- **The essay itself**: likely disclosable.
- **Past behavioral reports**: often disclosable if relevant to assessing or responding to the threat.
- **Counseling notes**: depends on what they are.

### Special caution on counseling notes
If by “counseling notes” you mean:
1. **Education records maintained by the school** relating to the student, they may be disclosed under the emergency exception if necessary.
2. **Sole possession notes** kept only as a personal memory aid and not shared with others, those may not be “education records” under FERPA at all.
3. If the student is 18+ or postsecondary and the records are treatment records, different rules may apply—but for a high school student, that usually is not the main issue.

Also remember: FERPA is not the only law in play. **State confidentiality laws, school policy, mandated reporting duties, and threat assessment protocols** may matter too.

### Best practical FERPA approach
If there appears to be an immediate threat:

1. **Notify school administration and your threat assessment/school safety team immediately.**
2. **Disclose only what law enforcement needs** to respond to the threat.
3. **Document the basis** for concluding there was a health/safety emergency.
4. **Record the disclosure** as required by FERPA’s recordkeeping provisions.

### Why this is allowed
The statutory basis is:
- **20 U.S.C. § 1232g(b)(1)(I)** — disclosure “in connection with an emergency” to appropriate persons if necessary to protect health or safety.
- FERPA also allows schools to include and share disciplinary/safety-related information with officials having legitimate educational interests under **§ 1232g(h)**, but for police disclosure, the emergency exception is the key one.

### Bottom line
**Yes, FERPA can allow disclosure to local law enforcement without parental consent if the school reasonably believes there is a serious health or safety emergency.**  
But disclose **only information necessary to address the threat**, and document why the emergency exception was invoked.
