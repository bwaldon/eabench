Query:

"One of our admissions staff accidentally emailed a student's full transcript to the wrong family — a different applicant with a similar name. We caught it within an hour and asked the recipient to delete it. The student whose transcript went out is furious, says we 'violated FERPA,' and insists we will lose our federal funding over it. Are they right?"

Statutory provision

20 U.S.C. § 1232g(b)(1) No funds shall be made available under any applicable program to any educational agency or institution which has a policy or practice of permitting the release of education records (or personally identifiable information contained therein other than directory information …) of students without the written consent of their parents to any individual, agency, or organization, other than to [the enumerated exceptions].

20 U.S.C. § 1232g(f) The Secretary shall take appropriate actions to enforce this section and to deal with violations of this section, in accordance with this chapter, except that action to terminate assistance may be taken only if the Secretary finds there has been a failure to comply with this section, and he has determined that compliance cannot be secured by voluntary means.

20 U.S.C. § 1232g(g) The Secretary shall establish or designate an office and review board within the Department for the purpose of investigating, processing, reviewing, and adjudicating violations of this section and complaints which may be filed concerning alleged violations of this section.

Expected follow-up question:

Was this an isolated, inadvertent disclosure by an individual employee, or does it reflect an institutional policy or practice of permitting unauthorized releases? And has the institution taken steps to address the error and prevent recurrence?

FERPA's funding sanction under § 1232g(b)(1) attaches only to an institution that has a "policy or practice" of permitting unauthorized disclosure — not to a single, inadvertent act by an employee. Enforcement runs through the Secretary and the Department's review board (§ 1232g(f), (g)), and funds may be terminated only after a finding of non-compliance that cannot be secured by voluntary means. The threshold question is therefore whether the incident is systemic or a one-off, and what remediation has occurred.

Query Continuation

"It was a one-time human error. We have a written FERPA policy, staff are trained annually, and transcript access is restricted. We have confirmed the email was deleted, documented the incident, and re-checked our send procedures. Nothing like this has happened before."

Expected Final Answer

The student is not correct that a single mistaken email puts the college in violation of FERPA or in jeopardy of losing federal funds. The funding sanction under § 1232g(b)(1) is triggered by a "policy or practice" of permitting the unauthorized release of education records, not by an isolated, inadvertent disclosure by an employee — and here the facts (a written policy, annual training, restricted access, prompt remediation, and no prior incidents) show an isolated error rather than a practice. Even where a violation is found, enforcement is administrative: the Department's office and review board investigates and adjudicates complaints (§ 1232g(g)), and the Secretary may terminate assistance only after determining that compliance cannot be secured by voluntary means (§ 1232g(f)) — so loss of funding is a last resort, not an automatic consequence of one disclosure. The institution should nonetheless treat the incident seriously, as it has: confirm deletion, document what happened, notify as appropriate, and reinforce procedures — precisely so that a recurring pattern does not harden into the "practice" that would create real exposure. If, by contrast, misdirected disclosures were happening repeatedly and the school was not correcting them, that could amount to a policy or practice and draw Department enforcement.

Concepts in explanation

- POLICY-OR-PRACTICE THRESHOLD: The funding sanction reaches a policy or practice of unauthorized release, not a single inadvertent disclosure.
- ADMINISTRATIVE ENFORCEMENT: FERPA is enforced by the Department (§ 1232g(f), (g)); termination of funds is a last resort available only after voluntary compliance fails.
- REMEDIATION MATTERS: Prompt correction keeps an isolated error from ripening into a "practice."
