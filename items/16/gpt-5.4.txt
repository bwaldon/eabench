Yes. FERPA permits the disclosure in this situation, even without the student’s consent and even if the student objects.

Why:

- Under FERPA, once a student is 18 or attends a postsecondary institution, FERPA rights transfer from the parent to the student. That is the general rule. See subsection (d).
- But FERPA contains a specific exception for alcohol and controlled substance violations by students under 21 at institutions of higher education. See subsection (i)(1).

That provision says an institution of higher education may disclose to a parent or legal guardian information regarding a violation of:
- Federal, State, or local law, or
- any rule or policy of the institution,
governing the use or possession of alcohol or a controlled substance,

if:
1. the student is under age 21, and
2. the institution determines that the student committed a disciplinary violation regarding that use or possession.

Applying that here:
- The student is 20.  
- The university is a postsecondary institution.  
- The institution determined through a disciplinary process that the student violated campus policy related to alcohol possession.  

That squarely fits subsection (i)(1). So FERPA allows disclosure to the parent or legal guardian notwithstanding the student’s lack of consent or express objection.

Important qualifiers:
- This is permissive, not mandatory. FERPA says the institution is not prohibited from disclosing; it does not require disclosure.
- The disclosure may concern the alcohol violation information. It is best to limit the disclosure to information reasonably related to the violation and the institution’s determination.
- Check state law and institutional policy. FERPA subsection (i)(2) says this provision does not override state law that prohibits the disclosure. So FERPA permits it unless state law bars it.
- Ensure the recipient is actually a parent or legal guardian.

Bottom line:
FERPA does not bar the university from notifying the parents here. The university may disclose the alcohol-possession disciplinary violation to the student’s parents despite the student’s objection, so long as the student is under 21 and no contrary state law prohibits the disclosure.
