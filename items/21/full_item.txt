Relevant Provision
(b)(1)(D)  No funds shall be made available under any applicable program to any educational agency or institution which has a policy or practice of permitting the release of education records (or personally identifiable information contained therein other than directory information, as defined in paragraph (5) of subsection (a)) of students without the written consent of their parents to any individual, agency, or organization, other than to the following— in connection with a student’s application for, or receipt of, financial aid.
(b)(4)(B)  With respect to this subsection, personal information shall only be transferred to a third party on the condition that such party will not permit any other party to have access to such information without the written consent of the parents of the student. If a third party outside the educational agency or institution permits access to information in violation of paragraph (2)(A), or fails to destroy information in violation of paragraph (1)(F), the educational agency or institution shall be prohibited from permitting access to information from education records to that third party for a period of not less than five years.
Query
I work in the financial aid office of a private university. A national scholarship foundation has asked us to send them an enrolled student’s academic transcript, disciplinary record, and a copy of the student’s most recent financial aid file. We do not have the student’s written consent. May we share the records under FERPA?
Expected follow-up question:
Is the requested information being used to determine the student’s eligibility for a scholarship, the amount of the award, the conditions attached to the award, or to enforce the terms or conditions of the aid — and has the student actually applied for or begun receiving aid from this foundation?
Query Continuation
Yes, the student has formally applied for the scholarship, and the foundation needs the academic transcript and disciplinary record to determine eligibility and the conditions of the award. The financial aid file was requested so the foundation can coordinate the scholarship with other awards the student is receiving.
Expected Final Answer
FERPA permits the disclosure under the financial aid exception in (b)(1)(D), but only insofar as the records are necessary in connection with the student’s application for or receipt of financial aid — specifically, to determine eligibility, the amount of the aid, the conditions of the aid, or to enforce the terms and conditions of the aid. The transcript and disciplinary record are reasonably related to eligibility and conditions, and the financial aid file is reasonably related to coordination of awards, so these disclosures fall within the exception.
Two important caveats apply. First, the exception is purpose-limited: you should disclose only the information that is actually necessary for the foundation’s eligibility, amount, conditions, or enforcement determinations, not the student’s entire file. Second, under (b)(4)(B), the foundation as a third-party recipient is prohibited from redisclosing the information to any other party without the written consent of the student. If they fail to comply, they must not be permitted to access information from your institution’s education records, for a period of not less than five years.
