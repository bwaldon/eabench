Usually **no, not without consent**, unless a specific FERPA exception applies.

## Short answer
A police department does **not automatically** get access to a student’s education records. Under FERPA, you generally need:

1. **Written consent** from the parent or eligible student, **or**
2. A valid **FERPA exception**, such as:
   - a **lawfully issued subpoena or court order**
   - a genuine **health or safety emergency**
   - records that are **not FERPA education records** in the first place, such as certain **law enforcement unit records**

## Key FERPA rules
Under **20 U.S.C. § 1232g(b)(1)–(2)**, schools may not disclose education records or personally identifiable information without consent unless an exception applies.

## When you may disclose to police

### 1. Subpoena or court order
You may disclose if the request is backed by a **judicial order or lawfully issued subpoena**.  
FERPA generally requires **advance notice to the parent or eligible student** before complying, unless:

- it is a **federal grand jury subpoena**, or
- another law-enforcement subpoena/order specifically directs **non-disclosure**, or
- it is in certain child abuse/neglect or dependency proceedings.

Relevant text: **§ 1232g(b)(2)(B)** and **§ 1232g(b)(1)(J)**.

### 2. Health or safety emergency
You may disclose to **appropriate persons**, including police, if knowledge of the information is **necessary to protect the health or safety of the student or others**.  
Relevant text: **§ 1232g(b)(1)(I)**.

This is typically interpreted narrowly: there should be an **articulable and significant threat**.

### 3. Law enforcement unit records
If your institution has a FERPA-defined **law enforcement unit**, records **created by that unit for law enforcement purposes** are generally **not education records** under FERPA.  
Relevant text: **§ 1232g(a)(4)(B)(ii)**.

So if the police are asking for:
- a campus police incident report created and maintained by the campus police for law enforcement purposes, FERPA may not restrict disclosure of that record.
- but if they are asking for registrar, disciplinary, attendance, grade, or counseling records, those are often FERPA-protected education records.

### 4. Terrorism-related court order
There is also a special provision allowing disclosure pursuant to a court order in certain terrorism investigations.  
Relevant text: **§ 1232g(j)**.

## When you generally may not disclose
If the police simply ask informally for:
- transcripts
- grades
- attendance
- discipline file
- class schedule
- address from the student file
- advising or academic records

then **FERPA usually prohibits disclosure** unless consent or a specific exception applies.

## Important distinction: directory information
Some limited information may be released if your school has properly designated it as **directory information** and the student has **not opted out**.  
Relevant text: **§ 1232g(a)(5)**.

But be careful: whether something is directory information depends on your institution’s published FERPA notice and opt-out process.

## Practical rule
Ask:

1. **What exact records are being requested?**
2. **Are they education records?**
3. **Do we have student/parent consent?**
4. **Is there a subpoena/court order?**
5. **Is there a true health/safety emergency?**
6. **Are these law enforcement unit records instead of education records?**

## Best practice response
If police request records, do **not** release immediately unless your FERPA officer/counsel confirms a valid basis. You can respond:

> “Student records are protected by FERPA. We can review your request upon receipt of written consent, a valid subpoena/court order, or if a recognized FERPA emergency exception applies.”

## Bottom line
**No, you do not have to provide student records to a police department just because they ask.**  
You may provide them **only if**:
- you have **consent**, or
- a **FERPA exception** applies, most commonly a **subpoena/court order**, **health/safety emergency**, or the records are **law enforcement unit records** rather than education records.
