Query:

"A police department is asking us for a student's records. Do we have to provide them?"

Statutory provision 
20 U.S.C. § 1232g(b)(1)(E) No funds shall be made available under any applicable program to any educational agency or institution which has a policy or practice of permitting the release of education records (or personally identifiable information contained therein other than directory information, as defined in paragraph (5) of subsection (a)) of students without the written consent of their parents to any individual, agency, or organization, other than to the following —
(E): State and local officials or authorities to whom such information is specifically allowed to be reported or disclosed pursuant to State statute adopted — (i) before November 19, 1974, if the allowed reporting or disclosure concerns the juvenile justice system and such system's ability to effectively serve the student whose records are released; or (ii) after November 19, 1974, if — (I) the allowed reporting or disclosure concerns the juvenile justice system and such system's ability to effectively serve, before adjudication, the student whose records are released; and (II) the officials and authorities to whom such information is disclosed certify in writing to the educational agency or institution that the information will not be disclosed to any other party except as provided under State law without the prior written consent of the parent of the student.
20 U.S.C. § 1232g(b)(1)(J) Nothing contained in this section shall be construed to prohibit educational agencies or institutions from disclosing education records to a court in compliance with a lawfully issued subpoena, provided the educational agency or institution makes a reasonable effort to notify the parent or eligible student of the order or subpoena in advance of compliance. 
Expected follow-up question:
What is the legal basis for the request?  Is the police department presenting a court order or subpoena, requesting records under a state juvenile justice statute, or making an informal request?
A state juvenile justice statute may authorise disclosure, but its date of adoption determines which conditions apply: pre-1974 statutes carry fewer requirements than post-1974 ones. An informal request with no legal basis does not authorise disclosure without consent. The model cannot provide correct advice without knowing which situation applies.
Query Continuation
"The police department has not presented a subpoena or court order. They say they are investigating a juvenile matter involving the student and believe the records are relevant.”
Expected Final Answer
Without a subpoena, court order, or applicable state juvenile justice statute specifically authorising disclosure, FERPA does not permit the LEA to release the student's records to the police department based on an informal request. The general rule under FERPA is that disclosure without written parental consent is prohibited. The juvenile justice exception under §1232g(b)(1)(E) applies only where a state statute specifically authorizes the disclosure and the state statute's date of adoption determines which conditions must be met. For statutes adopted before November 19, 1974, the disclosure must concern the juvenile justice system's ability to serve the student effectively. For statutes adopted after November 19, 1974, two additional conditions apply: the disclosure must concern the system's ability to serve the student before adjudication, and the receiving officials must provide a written certification to the LEA that the information will not be further disclosed except as provided under state law without prior written parental consent. None of these conditions is triggered by an informal police request. The LEA should decline and advise the police department to obtain a subpoena or identify the specific state statute authorising disclosure, at which point the LEA can assess whether the applicable conditions are satisfied.
