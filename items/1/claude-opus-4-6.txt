# Police Requests for Student Records Under FERPA

## Short Answer

**No, you generally do NOT have to provide them**, and in most cases, you **cannot** release education records to a police department without proper authorization. FERPA restricts disclosure of education records and personally identifiable information.

## When You **Can** Disclose to Law Enforcement

There are only specific circumstances under which disclosure is permissible:

### 1. **Written Parental/Student Consent** — §(b)(1)/(b)(2)(A)
- The parent (or eligible student if 18+/postsecondary) provides written consent specifying the records, reasons, and recipient.

### 2. **Judicial Order or Lawfully Issued Subpoena** — §(b)(2)(B)
- You **may** comply with a valid court order or subpoena, **but** you must:
  - Notify the parent and student **in advance** of compliance
  - **Exception:** A court may order the institution *not* to disclose the existence of the subpoena for good cause (§(b)(1)(J)(ii))

### 3. **Health or Safety Emergency** — §(b)(1)(I)
- Disclosure to "appropriate persons" is permitted if knowledge of the information is **necessary to protect the health or safety** of the student or others
- This is narrowly construed and subject to the Secretary's regulations
- Must be a genuine, immediate emergency

### 4. **Attorney General — Terrorism Investigation** — §(j)
- The Attorney General can obtain an **ex parte court order** for records relevant to terrorism investigations
- This requires specific and articulable facts and a court order

### 5. **Law Enforcement Unit Records Exception** — §(a)(4)(B)(ii)
- Records **created and maintained by the institution's own law enforcement unit** for law enforcement purposes are **not considered education records** under FERPA and can be shared more freely
- This does **not** apply to regular education records

### 6. **Grand Jury Subpoena** — §(b)(1)(J)(i)
- A federal grand jury subpoena compels production, and the court may impose a gag order on the institution

## Key Points to Remember

- **A routine police request — even a formal letter — is NOT sufficient.** Without a subpoena, court order, consent, or applicable exception, you must decline.
- **Directory information** (name, address, etc.) may be releasable if your institution has designated it as such and followed proper public notice procedures under §(a)(5) — but even this has limits.
- You must **maintain a record of access** documenting who requested and obtained records and their legitimate interest — §(b)(4)(A).
- Violations can result in **termination of federal funding** — §(f).

## Recommended Steps

1. **Ask the police department for the legal basis** of their request (subpoena, court order, etc.)
2. **Consult your institution's legal counsel** before releasing anything
3. **If a valid subpoena/court order exists**, notify the parent/eligible student before complying (unless a court has ordered otherwise)
4. **Document everything** in the student's access record

> **Bottom line:** A police request alone, without a court order, subpoena, parental consent, or a qualifying emergency, does **not** obligate or permit you to release education records under FERPA.
