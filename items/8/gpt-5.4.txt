Yes—sometimes, but not automatically.

Under FERPA, a school may disclose education records in response to a judicial order or a lawfully issued subpoena without parental consent, but the key conditions matter.

## General FERPA rule
Under 20 U.S.C. § 1232g(b)(2)(B), you may disclose records:

- **in compliance with a judicial order**, or
- **pursuant to a lawfully issued subpoena**

**if** you first make a reasonable effort to notify the parent (or the student, if the student is an eligible student) **in advance of compliance**, so they have a chance to seek protective action.

## Important exceptions to the notice requirement
You generally **do not notify** the parent/student if:

1. **Federal grand jury subpoena**  
   If the subpoena/court order says you must not disclose the existence or contents of the subpoena.

2. **Other law enforcement subpoena with a nondisclosure order**  
   If the court or issuing agency has ordered, for good cause, that you not disclose it.

3. **Certain child abuse/neglect or dependency proceedings**  
   If a parent is a party to such a proceeding and the order is issued in that context, additional notice to that parent is not required.

## Practical answer for your school
Before handing over records, you should confirm:

- Is this a **valid judicial order or lawfully issued subpoena**?
- Does it include a **gag/nondisclosure order**?
- Who is the student:
  - if under 18 and not postsecondary: notify the **parent**
  - if 18+ or in postsecondary: notify the **student**
- Is the subpoena limited to specific records?  
  You should disclose **only what is required**, not the full file unless demanded.

## Best practice steps
1. **Do not ignore the subpoena.**
2. **Send it immediately to district counsel/school attorney.**
3. **Verify authenticity and scope.**
4. **Check for any nondisclosure language.**
5. **If no gag order applies, give advance notice** to the parent/eligible student before producing records.
6. **Log the disclosure** in the student’s FERPA access record, unless a special exception applies.
7. Produce only the records actually required.

## Bottom line
**Yes, FERPA permits disclosure in response to a valid subpoena or court order, but usually only after advance notice to the parent or eligible student—unless the subpoena/order specifically prohibits notice or falls within a statutory exception.**
