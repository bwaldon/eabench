The short answer is **yes, you can (and generally must) hand over the records in response to a lawfully issued subpoena, but you usually cannot do so immediately.** 

Under FERPA, specifically **Section (b)(2)(B)**, schools are permitted to disclose personally identifiable information from education records without parental consent if the disclosure is in compliance with a lawfully issued subpoena. However, FERPA imposes a strict procedural requirement you must follow before handing anything over. 

Here is what you need to do step-by-step:

### 1. The Advance Notification Rule
Unless an exception applies (see step 2), FERPA requires your school to make a reasonable effort to **notify the student's parents of the subpoena *in advance* of complying with it.** 
* *Why?* This advance notice gives the parents an opportunity to seek a protective order or file a motion to quash the subpoena in court before the records are released. 
* *(Note: Because this is a middle school, the student is almost certainly under 18. Under Section (d), FERPA rights belong to the parents until the student turns 18 or enters a postsecondary institution).*

### 2. Check for Exceptions to the Notification Rule
You must carefully read the subpoena to see if it prohibits you from notifying the parents. You are **exempt from the advance notification requirement** only under the following specific circumstances:
* **Federal Grand Jury Subpoena [Section (b)(1)(J)(i)]:** The subpoena is from a federal grand jury, and the court has specifically ordered that the existence or contents of the subpoena not be disclosed.
* **Law Enforcement Subpoena [Section (b)(1)(J)(ii)]:** The subpoena is issued for a law enforcement purpose, and the court or issuing agency has explicitly ordered the school not to disclose the existence or contents of the subpoena.
* **Child Abuse, Neglect, or Dependency Proceeding [Section (b)(2)(B)]:** The parent is a party to a court proceeding involving child abuse, neglect, or dependency matters, and the subpoena is issued in the context of that specific proceeding.
* **Terrorism Investigations [Section (j)]:** The order is an *ex parte* order obtained by the Attorney General for an authorized investigation or prosecution of terrorism. 

### 3. Record the Disclosure
If/when you do release the records, remember your record-keeping obligations under **Section (b)(4)(A)**. You must keep a record with the student's education files indicating who obtained access to the records and their legitimate interest in doing so (in this case, noting the court/agency that issued the subpoena). *Note: This logging requirement does not apply if the order is for a terrorism investigation under Section (j).*

### Summary Checklist for the Principal:
1. **Review the subpoena:** Does it explicitly order you *not* to notify the parents, or is it related to a child abuse/neglect case where the parent is a party? 
2. **If YES:** Do not notify the parents. Comply with the subpoena and provide the records by the deadline.
3. **If NO:** You must notify the parents that you have received a subpoena for their child's records. Give them a reasonable amount of time to challenge the subpoena in court. If they do not successfully quash the subpoena before the compliance deadline, you may hand over the records.
