You’ve provided the core FERPA statute, codified at 20 U.S.C. § 1232g. At a high level, it does the following:

- Gives parents, and later eligible students, rights to:
  - inspect and review education records,
  - seek amendment of inaccurate or misleading records,
  - consent before most disclosures of personally identifiable information.
- Restricts educational agencies/institutions that receive federal funds from disclosing education records without consent, subject to specific exceptions.
- Defines key terms like:
  - education records,
  - directory information,
  - student,
  - educational agency or institution.
- Transfers FERPA rights from parents to the student when the student:
  - turns 18, or
  - attends a postsecondary institution.
- Provides exceptions for disclosure, including:
  - school officials with legitimate educational interests,
  - transfer schools,
  - financial aid,
  - health or safety emergencies,
  - certain subpoenas/court orders,
  - audits/evaluations,
  - dependent students,
  - certain child welfare and juvenile justice disclosures,
  - certain disciplinary results,
  - alcohol/drug violation disclosures to parents of under-21 college students.

A few especially important FERPA points from this text:

1. Access rights
- Parents of K–12 students generally have the right to inspect records within 45 days.
- If a record contains information about multiple students, the parent only gets the portion relating to their child.

2. Amendment/hearing rights
- Schools must provide a hearing process to challenge records as inaccurate, misleading, or privacy-violating.
- FERPA does not require changing records just because a parent disagrees; it requires a process and allows a written statement to be added.

3. Consent rule
- Personally identifiable information from education records generally cannot be disclosed without written consent unless an exception applies.

4. What is not an education record
Notably excluded:
- sole-possession notes,
- law-enforcement-unit records created for law enforcement purposes,
- certain employment records,
- certain treatment records for adult/postsecondary students.

5. Directory information
- Schools may disclose directory information only if they gave public notice of what they designate as directory information and allowed an opt-out.

6. Eligible student rule
- Once the student is 18 or in postsecondary education, rights belong to the student, not the parent, unless another FERPA exception applies, such as dependency.

7. Enforcement
- FERPA is enforced by the U.S. Department of Education, primarily through administrative enforcement tied to federal funding.
