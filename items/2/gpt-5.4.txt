Probably yes, if the access is structured correctly under FERPA’s **“school official” exception**. If not, then no.

## Short answer
A state education agency or school may allow a **third-party vendor’s engineers** to access personally identifiable student information **without parental consent** only if the vendor is treated as a FERPA-authorized party under the statute/regulations—most commonly as a **school official with legitimate educational interests** acting for the agency/institution.

From the text you provided, FERPA allows disclosure without consent to:

- **“other school officials… who have been determined… to have legitimate educational interests”**  
  20 U.S.C. § 1232g(b)(1)(A)

and also imposes limits on redisclosure:

- personal information transferred to a third party must be on the condition that the third party **will not permit any other party to have access** without required consent  
  § 1232g(b)(4)(B)

## What this means for your vendor engineers
Vendor engineers are **not automatically** allowed access just because they support your platform. Their access is generally compliant only if all of the following are true:

1. **The vendor is performing an institutional/agency service or function** you would otherwise use your own employees to perform.
2. The vendor is under the **direct control** of the SEA regarding the use and maintenance of education records.
3. The engineers have access only because they have a **legitimate educational interest**—here, maintenance/troubleshooting of the student data platform can qualify if necessary to operate the system.
4. The contract or DPA strictly limits the vendor’s use of the data to the authorized purpose.
5. The vendor is prohibited from **redisclosing** the data except as FERPA permits.
6. The agency maintains required **recordkeeping** of disclosures/access where applicable.

## Important SEA-specific caution
Because you are a **state education agency**, the analysis depends in part on:
- whose records these are,
- whether the SEA itself maintains them, and
- whether the SEA is acting within FERPA’s audit/evaluation/enforcement framework or as an operator of a system for LEAs/schools.

FERPA does permit parents to inspect records maintained by a **State educational agency**. The statute also permits certain disclosures to authorized representatives and contractors in audit/evaluation contexts, but that is a different path than ordinary vendor operations.

For a vendor operating your platform, the cleanest FERPA theory is usually:
- the vendor is a **school official/contractor** under FERPA regulations, or
- the vendor is acting as your **authorized representative** if the access is tied to audit/evaluation/enforcement functions.

Which one applies depends on the role of the platform and the purpose of access.

## When it would likely be noncompliant
You likely have a FERPA problem if:
- the vendor contract does **not** place the vendor under your direct control;
- engineers are using live student data for general product development, AI training, analytics, or unrelated debugging;
- access is broader than necessary;
- there are no confidentiality and redisclosure restrictions;
- access is not tied to a documented legitimate educational interest or authorized representative function;
- the vendor can keep the data indefinitely.

## Practical compliance checklist
Before allowing access, make sure you have:

- A written contract/DPA stating the vendor is performing a service for the SEA
- Clear statement that access is only for maintenance/troubleshooting
- “Direct control” terms over use, disclosure, security, retention, and destruction
- Redisclosure prohibition
- Need-to-know / least-privilege access controls
- Logging/audit trail of engineer access
- Data minimization, masking where possible
- Incident response and confidentiality obligations
- Data destruction/return provisions after issue resolution
- Public FERPA notice, if relying on the school-official exception, that identifies contractors/outsourcers as school officials where applicable

## Bottom line
**Yes, potentially**—you can be in compliance **without parental consent** if the vendor engineers’ access falls within FERPA’s non-consent exception, most likely as **school officials with legitimate educational interests** and under your **direct control**, with strict contractual and operational safeguards.

**No, not automatically**—if the vendor is just a third party with broad access to identifiable student data and no proper FERPA structure, then the access would not be compliant.
