Yes—sometimes, but not automatically.

Under FERPA, a school may disclose education records in response to a judicial order or a lawfully issued subpoena without parent consent, but there are important conditions.

Key FERPA rule  
20 U.S.C. § 1232g(b)(2)(B) allows disclosure:

- in compliance with a judicial order, or
- pursuant to a lawfully issued subpoena,

if the school gives advance notice to the parent and student before complying, so they have a chance to object,

except in certain situations.

Main exceptions to the notice requirement  
You generally do not have to notify in advance if:

1. Federal grand jury subpoena  
   If it is a federal grand jury subpoena and the court orders nondisclosure.

2. Law-enforcement subpoena with nondisclosure order  
   If it is another subpoena issued for a law-enforcement purpose and the court or issuing agency orders you not to disclose its existence or contents.

3. Certain child abuse/neglect or dependency proceedings  
   If a parent is a party to a court proceeding involving child abuse and neglect or dependency matters, and the order was issued in that proceeding, additional notice to the parent is not required.

What you should do as principal  
Before releasing anything, confirm:

- Is it actually a valid subpoena or court order?
- Who issued it?
- Does it include a nondisclosure/gag order?
- Is it for law enforcement, civil litigation, custody, abuse/neglect, etc.?
- Who is the “eligible student”? If the student is 18 or attending postsecondary, notice/rights belong to the student, not the parent.

Best practice steps

1. Send it to district counsel immediately.  
   Do not respond on your own if you can avoid it.

2. Verify validity.  
   Check whether it is signed/issued properly and served correctly under state law.

3. Determine whether FERPA notice is required.  
   If no FERPA exception applies, provide advance notice before producing records.

4. Produce only what is required.  
   Don’t send the entire cumulative file unless the subpoena actually requires it.

5. Keep a record of disclosure.  
   FERPA generally requires maintaining a record of who requested/received access and their legitimate interest, though not in the terrorism-order situation.

Short answer  
- Yes, FERPA can permit disclosure in response to a subpoena.  
- But unless a specific exception applies, you must give advance notice to the parent (or eligible student) before complying.  
- If there is a nondisclosure order, follow that and do not notify.  
- In practice, have your district lawyer review it first.
