# Disclosing Education Records in Response to a Subpoena Under FERPA

Yes, FERPA does permit disclosure of education records in response to a lawfully issued subpoena, **but you must satisfy specific conditions before handing over the records.** Let me walk you through the relevant provisions carefully.

---

## The General Rule: Consent Required

Under **subsection (b)(1)**, the default rule is that education records (or personally identifiable information other than directory information) **cannot be released without written parental consent**. A subpoena, however, falls under one of the recognized exceptions.

## The Subpoena Exception: Subsection (b)(2)(B)

This provision allows disclosure pursuant to a **judicial order or lawfully issued subpoena**, but **only on the condition that:**

> *"parents and the students are notified of all such orders or subpoenas **in advance of the compliance therewith** by the educational institution or agency."*

### So your obligations are:

1. **Verify the subpoena is lawfully issued** — confirm it comes from a court or entity with proper authority
2. **Notify the student's parents before you comply** — this is mandatory, not optional, so they have an opportunity to seek to quash or modify the subpoena if they wish
3. **Then produce the records** in accordance with the subpoena's terms

---

## Critical Exceptions to the Advance Notice Requirement

There are **two situations** where you do **not** need to provide advance parental notice:

### 1. Federal Grand Jury Subpoenas — Subsection (b)(1)(J)(i)
If the subpoena is from a **federal grand jury** and the court has issued a **gag order for good cause shown**, you:
- Must comply with the subpoena
- Are **prohibited** from disclosing to anyone (including the parents) the existence or contents of the subpoena

### 2. Law Enforcement Subpoenas — Subsection (b)(1)(J)(ii)
If the subpoena was issued for a **law enforcement purpose**, the court or issuing agency **may** order you not to disclose the existence or contents of the subpoena to any person, again for good cause shown.

### 3. Child Abuse/Neglect/Dependency Proceedings
Under subsection (b)(2)(B), if:
- A **parent is a party** to a court proceeding involving **child abuse and neglect** or **dependency matters**, and
- The order is issued **in the context of that proceeding**

Then **additional notice to the parent is not required**.

---

## Recordkeeping Obligation

Under **subsection (b)(4)(A)**, you must also:

- **Document the disclosure** in a record maintained with the student's education records
- Note the party that requested or obtained access
- Note the **legitimate interest** that party has in the information

*(The only exception to this recordkeeping requirement is for disclosures subject to a court order under subsection (j), relating to terrorism investigations.)*

---

## Practical Steps for Your Situation

| Step | Action |
|------|--------|
| **1** | Review the subpoena to confirm it is lawfully issued and identify its source and purpose |
| **2** | Determine whether it falls under the grand jury, law enforcement, or child abuse exceptions |
| **3** | If **no exception** applies, **notify the parents in advance** of compliance so they may take legal action if desired |
| **4** | If an exception **does** apply (e.g., gag order), comply without parental notification as directed |
| **5** | Produce **only the records specified** in the subpoena — no more |
| **6** | Log the disclosure in the student's access record per subsection (b)(4)(A) |
| **7** | Consult your school district's legal counsel before producing anything |

---

## Bottom Line

**Yes, you can hand over the records — but not immediately.** Unless a specific exception applies, FERPA **requires advance notification to the parents** before you comply. Failure to follow this procedure could place your institution in violation of FERPA, potentially jeopardizing federal funding.

**I strongly recommend involving your district's legal counsel before responding to the subpoena** to ensure full compliance with both FERPA and any applicable state law requirements.
