# FERPA Analysis: Rights-Holder Determination and Notice Adequacy for a 17-Year-Old Postsecondary Student

## Executive Summary

This scenario presents two distinct legal questions that require careful analysis under FERPA. Perhaps counterintuitively, **the student—not the parent—is the primary FERPA rights-holder**, despite being a minor. However, the parent may still have an alternative pathway to access. Your general notice practice likely satisfies FERPA's minimum requirements but carries meaningful institutional risk that warrants remediation.

---

## I. Who Is the Rights-Holder?

### The Controlling Provision: Subsection (d)

The answer to this question turns on a single, critically important provision:

> *"For the purposes of this section, whenever a student has attained eighteen years of age, **or is attending an institution of postsecondary education**, the permission or consent required of and the rights accorded to the parents of the student shall thereafter only be required of and accorded to the student."*

This provision establishes **two independent, disjunctive triggers** for the transfer of FERPA rights from parent to student:

1. The student has turned 18, **OR**
2. The student is attending an institution of postsecondary education

Because your student is **enrolled full-time at a postsecondary institution**, the second trigger is satisfied regardless of the student's age. The statute is unambiguous on this point: the word "or" makes these alternative conditions, not cumulative ones.

### Practical Consequence

Once either trigger is met, FERPA rights transfer entirely to the student. This means:

- **The student**—not the parent—holds the right to inspect and review education records under subsection (a)(1)(A)
- **The student**—not the parent—must provide written consent before records are disclosed under subsection (b)(1)
- **The student**—not the parent—is entitled to a hearing to challenge record content under subsection (a)(2)
- **The student**—not the parent—must be informed of these rights under subsection (e)

### The Parent's Potential Alternative Pathways

While the parent is **not** the primary rights-holder, FERPA does not entirely foreclose parental access. Two provisions may apply:

#### Pathway 1: Dependency Exception — Subsection (b)(1)(H)

> Records may be disclosed without consent to *"parents of a dependent student of such parents, as defined in section 152 of title 26."*

If the parent claims the student as a **tax dependent** under 26 U.S.C. § 152, the university **may** (but is not required to) disclose education records to the parent without the student's consent. Key considerations:

- This is a **permissive** exception, not a mandatory one—the institution retains discretion
- The parent should be asked to provide **documentation of dependency status** (e.g., a copy of the relevant tax return or a signed declaration)
- This exception allows *disclosure* but does **not** transfer the underlying FERPA *rights* (inspection, hearing, consent) to the parent

#### Pathway 2: Student Consent — Subsection (b)(2)(A)

The student may provide **written consent** specifying:
- Which records may be released
- The reasons for release
- To whom the records will be released

This is the cleanest and most straightforward mechanism.

### Recommended Response to the Parent

The university should communicate clearly to the parent that:

1. Under FERPA subsection (d), rights have transferred to the student upon enrollment at the postsecondary institution, irrespective of the student's age
2. The parent may gain access through the **dependency exception** if applicable, subject to institutional policy and appropriate documentation
3. Alternatively, the parent may request that the **student provide written consent** for disclosure
4. The university is not legally obligated to grant access based solely on the parent-child relationship or the student's minor status

## II. Does the General Notice Practice Satisfy FERPA?

### The Statutory Requirement: Subsection (e)

> *"No funds shall be made available under any applicable program to any educational agency or institution unless such agency or institution **effectively informs** the parents of students, or the students, if they are eighteen years of age or older, or are attending an institution of postsecondary education, of the rights accorded them by this section."*

### Analysis of Your Current Practice

Your institution publishes FERPA notice online and includes it in orientation materials. Let us evaluate this against the statutory standard.

#### What the Statute Requires

The operative standard is **"effectively informs."** This is a functional standard, not a formalistic one. The institution must ensure that the appropriate rights-holder *actually receives meaningful notice* of their FERPA rights.

#### Who Must Be Informed

Under subsection (e), read in conjunction with subsection (d):

| Student Category | Who Must Be Informed |
|---|---|
| Students 18 or older | The **student** |
| Students attending postsecondary education (any age) | The **student** |
| Students under 18 at K-12 schools | The **parents** |

For your 17-year-old undergraduate, **the student is the person who must be effectively informed**, not the parent.

#### Does Your Practice Meet the Standard?

Your general notice practice **likely satisfies the minimum statutory threshold**, but with significant caveats:

**Arguments that the practice is sufficient:**
- Publishing the notice online and including it in orientation materials constitutes a reasonable method of reaching enrolled students
- FERPA does not prescribe a specific delivery mechanism
- Most institutions rely on similar general notice practices, and the Department of Education has generally accepted such approaches

**Arguments that the practice is vulnerable:**

1. **Lack of age-specific guidance creates confusion.** Your notice does not distinguish between students under and over 18. A 17-year-old student may reasonably assume their parents retain rights, thereby undermining the "effectively informs" standard. If the student does not understand that *they*—not their parents—hold the rights, have they truly been "effectively informed" of the rights "accorded them"?

2. **The parent's claim of ignorance is revealing.** The parent states they were never informed of FERPA rights. While subsection (e) requires notice to the *student* (not the parent) in this context, the parent's confusion suggests a systemic communication gap. If the parent is confused, the 17-year-old student may well be equally confused.

3. **Passive publication may not constitute "effective" notice for a distinctive population.** A 17-year-old first-year student is in an unusual situation. General online publication, while adequate for the typical student population, may not "effectively inform" a minor student who has no prior experience with the postsecondary education system and may not think to look for such information.

## III. Comprehensive Recommendations

### Immediate Actions

1. **Respond to the parent** by explaining the rights transfer under subsection (d), offering the dependency exception pathway under subsection (b)(1)(H) if applicable, and suggesting the student consent mechanism under subsection (b)(2)(A)

2. **Confirm that the student has received actual notice** of their FERPA rights. If there is any doubt, provide the student with a direct, individualized communication explaining their rights as the FERPA rights-holder

3. **Do not release records to the parent** until one of the legally authorized pathways (dependency documentation, student consent, or another subsection (b)(1) exception) has been properly established

### Systemic Policy Improvements

4. **Revise your FERPA notice to specifically address students under 18** who are enrolled at the postsecondary level. The notice should clearly state that FERPA rights transfer to the student upon enrollment, regardless of age, and explain what this means in practical terms

5. **Develop a targeted notification process for minor students** during admission or enrollment that includes:
   - A clear written explanation that FERPA rights belong to the student, not the parent
   - Information about the dependency exception and student consent mechanisms
   - An opportunity for the student to ask questions

6. **Create a parallel communication to parents of minor students** that explains:
   - The rights transfer under subsection (d)
   - The pathways through which they may still obtain access
   - Your institution's policies regarding the dependency exception

7. **Establish an institutional policy on the dependency exception** — decide whether your institution will exercise its discretion to disclose under subsection (b)(1)(H), and under what conditions and documentation requirements

8. **Document all notice efforts** to create an evidentiary record demonstrating compliance with the "effectively informs" standard under subsection (e)

---

## IV. Conclusion

The statutory text is clear: **your 17-year-old enrolled student is the FERPA rights-holder**, and the parent's rights have transferred by operation of subsection (d).
