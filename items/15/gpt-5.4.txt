Under FERPA, in your scenario, the **student** is the rights-holder, not the parent.

### 1. Who holds the FERPA rights here?
FERPA provides that:

> whenever a student **has attained 18 years of age, _or is attending an institution of postsecondary education_**, the rights otherwise accorded to parents transfer to the student.  
See **20 U.S.C. § 1232g(d)**.

Because this individual is **attending a postsecondary institution**, FERPA rights have transferred to the student **even though the student is only 17**.

So as a default rule:

- the **student** controls access to the education records;
- the **parent** does **not** automatically have a right of access merely because the student is a minor.

### 2. Can the parent ever get access?
Yes, but only through a FERPA exception or the student’s consent. The most relevant one is:

- disclosure to **parents of a dependent student**, as defined in **Internal Revenue Code § 152**  
  See **20 U.S.C. § 1232g(b)(1)(H)**.

That means if your institution has documentation or a policy-based process showing the student is the parent’s tax dependent, the university **may** disclose to the parent under that exception. FERPA generally makes this **permissive**, not mandatory.

Other possible bases could include:

- the student’s written consent;
- a health or safety emergency, if applicable;
- alcohol/drug violation disclosures for students under 21, if the statutory conditions are met.

But absent an exception, the parent is not the FERPA rights-holder here.

---

## 3. Does your general FERPA notice satisfy the duty to inform?
Likely **yes**, assuming it is actually provided effectively.

FERPA requires institutions to **effectively inform**:

- the **parents of students**, or
- the **students**, if they are **18 or older** **or attending a postsecondary institution**,  
of the rights accorded by FERPA.  
See **20 U.S.C. § 1232g(e)**.

Since this is a postsecondary student, the institution’s duty is to inform the **student**, not the parent.

### Your notice practice
You said the university:

- has a general FERPA notice policy,
- publishes it online, and
- includes it in orientation materials.

That generally aligns with FERPA’s notice requirement, provided the notice is disseminated in a way that is reasonably calculated to reach students. FERPA does not require a separate notice format solely because some postsecondary students are under 18.

### Do you need to distinguish under-18 from over-18 students?
Not necessarily. Under FERPA, both:
- students **18 or older**, and
- students **attending a postsecondary institution**

are treated as the rights-holders. So a general notice directed to students at the postsecondary institution is ordinarily sufficient even if it does not separately call out minors.

What matters is whether the institution **effectively informs** the proper rights-holder. For your 17-year-old undergraduate, that is the **student**.

---

## 4. Practical conclusion
### You should:
- treat the **student** as the FERPA rights-holder;
- **not** give the parent access solely based on parent status;
- evaluate whether any disclosure exception applies, especially **tax dependency**;
- ensure your notice is directed to students and is effectively disseminated.

### On the notice issue:
Your general online/orientation FERPA notice **likely satisfies FERPA**, even without separately distinguishing students under 18 from those over 18, because for **all postsecondary attendees**, the student is the relevant rights-holder.
