Short answer: it depends on which report you mean.

### Key FERPA rule
Under **20 U.S.C. § 1232g(a)(4)(B)(ii)**, **records maintained by a law enforcement unit that were created by that law enforcement unit for the purpose of law enforcement are not “education records.”**  
So a **campus police report created and maintained for criminal/law-enforcement purposes** is generally **not subject to FERPA’s access rules**.

### Applying that here

#### 1. Police report created solely for law enforcement purposes
Example: theft investigation report in the campus police database.

- This is typically a **FERPA law-enforcement-unit record**, not an education record.
- Therefore:
  - FERPA **does not give the parent a right to inspect it**.
  - FERPA also **does not prohibit disclosure** of it; disclosure is governed by other law/policy, not FERPA.
- The campus police are therefore likely **wrong** if they say it is “protected under FERPA because it contains the student’s education record.” A genuine law-enforcement-unit record is excluded from the definition of education record.

#### 2. Report created for or shared with the disciplinary office
If the report, or a version of it, is used by the university to document **student conduct/disciplinary matters** and is maintained by the disciplinary office or another university office for non-law-enforcement purposes, that version is usually an **education record** under FERPA.

- Then FERPA access rights belong to the **student**, not the parent, because the child is **19** and apparently attends a postsecondary institution.  
  See **§ 1232g(d)**: once the student is 18 or attends postsecondary education, rights transfer to the student.
- So the parent has **no automatic FERPA access right** unless:
  - the student gives **written consent**, or
  - the parent claims the student as a **dependent** under **26 U.S.C. § 152**, allowing disclosure under **§ 1232g(b)(1)(H)**.

### Directory information does not help here
The registrar’s reliance on “directory/record disclosure provisions” is likely misplaced.

- **Directory information** is limited to things like name, address, dates of attendance, degrees, etc.  
- An incident report or disciplinary report is **not directory information**.

So the parent cannot get the report merely because FERPA allows disclosure of directory information.

### What the parent may be able to learn
If this concerns a **disciplinary proceeding for a crime of violence or nonforcible sex offense**, FERPA permits limited disclosure of the **final results** in certain circumstances under **§ 1232g(b)(6)**. But that is narrow and usually applies to victims and certain public disclosures by postsecondary institutions, not a broad parental right to the whole report.

### Best FERPA analysis
- **Police-only criminal investigation report**: not an education record; FERPA neither grants access nor blocks disclosure.
- **Disciplinary record maintained by student conduct/disciplinary office**: education record; access belongs to the **student**, not the parent, unless consent or dependency exception applies.
- **Same facts appearing in both systems** do not make the police copy an education record just because a disciplinary office also has related records.

### Bottom line
The university should separate the records by function:

1. **Law enforcement unit record** → outside FERPA’s education-record definition.  
2. **Student disciplinary record** → likely an education record under FERPA.  
3. Because the student is **19**, the **student** controls FERPA rights unless the parent qualifies under the **dependent-student exception** or has the student’s consent.
