# Analysis of Compliance Obligations Under an Ex Parte Court Order Pursuant to 20 U.S.C. § 1232g(j)

## Short Answer

**Yes, you must comply with the order.** However, your obligations toward the student are significantly altered compared to normal subpoena compliance, and you need to understand precisely why and what protections you retain.

---

## I. Authority and Validity of the Order

Section (j) creates a specific mechanism that **expressly overrides** the entirety of subsections (a) through (i) and any provision of state law. This is the most sweeping preemption clause in the entire statute. To be validly issued, the order must satisfy the following requirements:

### A. Procedural Prerequisites

1. **The applicant must be the Attorney General or a designated federal officer at or above the rank of Assistant Attorney General.** You should verify on the face of the order or accompanying application that it identifies the applicant and their authority. An application from an FBI field agent or a line AUSA alone, without proper delegation, would be facially deficient.

2. **The application must be written and submitted to a court of competent jurisdiction.** Verify that the issuing court has jurisdiction—typically a U.S. District Court.

3. **The order is ex parte.** This is critical: the statute explicitly contemplates that neither you nor the student will have been heard before the order issues. The ex parte nature is not a deficiency—it is by design.

4. **The application must contain a certification** that there are "specific and articulable facts giving reason to believe that the education records are likely to contain information" relevant to:
   - An authorized investigation or prosecution of an offense listed in **18 U.S.C. § 2332b(g)(5)(B)** (federal terrorism offenses), **or**
   - An act of **domestic or international terrorism** as defined in **18 U.S.C. § 2331**.

### B. Your Right to Examine the Order

While the statute does not create an explicit mechanism for you to challenge the order, you are entitled—and indeed obligated as counsel—to review the order for **facial validity**. You should confirm:

- The order is signed by a federal judge of competent jurisdiction.
- It references the correct statutory authority under § 1232g(j).
- It specifies the education records to be produced (the statute says records "relevant to" the investigation, so the order should have some bounding language; if it simply says "all records," the scope question is discussed below).

---

## II. Scope of Production: "Complete Education Records"

The order directs production of the **complete** education records. You must analyze whether this is consistent with the statute.

### A. What Constitutes "Education Records" Under § 1232g(a)(4)

Under subsection (a)(4)(A), education records means records, files, documents, and other materials that:

1. Contain information **directly related to the student**, and
2. Are **maintained by the institution or by a person acting for the institution**.

### B. Statutory Exclusions from "Education Records"

Even under a § 1232g(j) order, the **definition** of "education records" in subsection (a)(4)(B) still governs what constitutes an education record in the first place. Section (j) overrides the **protections** of subsections (a) through (i)—it does not redefine the term. Therefore, the following categories remain **outside the definition** and are not "education records" you are compelled to produce under this specific statutory authority:

| Category | Statutory Basis |
|---|---|
| Sole-possession records of instructional/supervisory/administrative personnel not shared with others (except a substitute) | (a)(4)(B)(i) |
| Records created and maintained by a law enforcement unit for law enforcement purposes | (a)(4)(B)(ii) |
| Employment records (for student-employees, only to the extent the records relate exclusively to employment capacity) | (a)(4)(B)(iii) |
| Treatment records made by physicians, psychiatrists, psychologists, or other professionals used only in connection with treatment | (a)(4)(B)(iv) |

**Practical note:** The DOJ may separately subpoena or obtain court orders for these categories under other legal authorities (grand jury subpoena, search warrant, etc.), but the FERPA § 1232g(j) order does not, by its own terms, reach them.

### C. Breadth Concerns

If you believe the order is overbroad—requesting truly everything rather than records "relevant to" the authorized investigation—you may consider filing a motion to quash or narrow, but you should weigh:

- The **ex parte** nature of the proceeding suggests Congress intended speed and secrecy.
- Section (j)(3) provides a **good-faith safe harbor**: an institution that "in good faith, produces education records in accordance with an order issued under this subsection shall not be liable to any person for that production."
- Delaying production to litigate scope carries risk if the court views your objection unfavorably, but it is not foreclosed by the statute.

---

## III. Obligations Toward the Student

This is the most consequential practical question, and the answer differs dramatically from ordinary subpoena compliance.

### A. The Normal Notification Requirement Is Overridden

Under subsection (b)(2)(B), the general rule is that when education records are disclosed pursuant to judicial order or subpoena, parents and students **must be notified in advance of compliance**. However:

**Section (j)(1) explicitly states: "Notwithstanding subsections (a) through (i)..."**

This preemption clause means the advance-notification requirement of (b)(2)(B) **does not apply**. The entire framework of subsection (b) is subordinated to the § (j) order.

### B. The Access-Log Requirement Is Expressly Suspended

Subsection (b)(4)(A) normally requires you to maintain a record of all individuals, agencies, or organizations that have requested or obtained access to a student's education records. However:

**Section (j)(4) explicitly provides: "Subsection (b)(4) does not apply to education records subject to a court order under this subsection."**

This means:
- You are **not required** to log the DOJ's access in the student's file.
- The student and parents have **no right** to discover, through the FERPA access log, that the production occurred.

### C. Can You Voluntarily Notify the Student?

This is the critical judgment call. The statute does not contain an explicit **gag order** provision analogous to, for example, National Security Letter statutes (18 U.S.C. § 2709) or the provision in § (b)(1)(J) regarding grand jury subpoenas where a court "shall order" non-disclosure.

**However**, you must consider:

1. **The ex parte nature of the order** strongly implies that Congress intended the target not to be informed. If Congress had intended notification, it would not have created an ex parte mechanism that specifically overrides the notification provisions.

2. **The court order itself may contain a non-disclosure provision.** Read the order carefully. Many § 1232g(j) orders include explicit gag provisions imposed under the court's inherent authority or under the broader statutory framework of the terrorism investigation. If the order contains such a provision, **you are bound by it**.

3. **Even absent an explicit gag provision in the order**, voluntary notification could constitute **obstruction of justice** (18 U.S.C. § 1503, § 1505, or § 1510) or could interfere with a terrorism investigation in ways that expose the university to criminal liability.

**My recommendation:** Do **not** notify the student unless and until you have confirmed with the issuing court or the DOJ that notification is permissible. If the order is silent on notification, seek clarification from the court before making any disclosure to the student.

### D. The Student's Rights Under Subsection (d)

Because this student is attending a postsecondary institution, under subsection (d), all parental rights have transferred to the student. Under normal circumstances, the student—not the parents—would be the person entitled to notification and consent. But as analyzed above, § (j) overrides these rights entirely for purposes of this order.

## IV. The Good-Faith Safe Harbor

Section (j)(3) provides:

> "An educational agency or institution that, in good faith, produces education records in accordance with an order issued under this subsection shall not be liable to any person for that production."

This is broad liability protection. "Any person" encompasses the student, parents, and third parties. "Not liable" encompasses both FERPA-based claims and, implicitly, state-law tort claims (since § (j)(1) also preempts state law).
