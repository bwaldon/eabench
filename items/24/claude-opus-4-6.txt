# FERPA Access Log Requirements: Your Obligations

## The Short Answer

**Yes, you are obligated to provide this information, and your district is almost certainly in violation of FERPA for not maintaining such a log in the first place.**

---

## The Statutory Basis

The requirement comes directly from **Section (b)(4)(A)**, which is unambiguous:

> "Each educational agency or institution **shall maintain** a record, kept with the education records of each student, which will indicate **all individuals** (other than those specified in paragraph (1)(A) of this subsection), **agencies, or organizations which have requested or obtained access** to a student's education records maintained by such educational agency or institution, and which will indicate specifically **the legitimate interest** that each such person, agency, or organization has in obtaining this information."

This is not discretionary language. The word "**shall**" creates a mandatory obligation.

---

## What the Law Requires You to Maintain

The access log must document:

1. **Every individual, agency, or organization** that requested or obtained access
2. **The specific legitimate interest** each requester had in obtaining the information
3. This record must be **kept with the education records** of each student

## The One Critical Exception

The statute carves out **one narrow category** that does **not** need to be logged:

> Those specified in **paragraph (1)(A)** — meaning "other school officials, including teachers within the educational institution or local educational agency, who have been determined by such agency or institution to have legitimate educational interests"

So internal school officials with legitimate educational interests are exempt from the logging requirement. **Everyone else must be logged.**

---

## Who Can See the Access Log?

Section (b)(4)(A) specifies that the access record shall be available only to:

- **Parents**
- **The school official (and assistants) responsible for custody of the records**
- **Authorized representatives** under clauses (A) and (C) of paragraph (1) — the Comptroller General, the Secretary, and State educational authorities — for auditing purposes

The parent in your scenario **has an explicit statutory right** to review this log.

---

## Your Immediate Problem

You face several compounding issues:

### 1. The Compliance Violation
Your district's failure to maintain these logs is itself a policy or practice that violates FERPA. Under **Section (f)**, the Secretary is empowered to take enforcement action when there has been "a failure to comply with this section." The absence of a centralized — or any — logging system means your district has been non-compliant for as long as you have lacked this practice.

### 2. The Parent's Request
Under **Section (a)(1)(A)**, the parent has a right to inspect and review her child's education records. The access log is, by statute, kept **with** the education records and is explicitly available to parents. You must respond within **45 days** of the request.

### 3. The Practical Difficulty
You cannot produce what you have not maintained. This creates a deeply uncomfortable situation: you have a legal obligation to provide a record that you have failed to create.

---

## Recommended Course of Action

### Immediate Steps

1. **Do not ignore or deny the parent's request.** Denying access is precisely the kind of policy or practice that can trigger a funding consequences analysis under Section (a)(1)(A).

2. **Conduct a good-faith reconstruction** of what access information you *can* determine. This may include:
   - Electronic access logs from your Student Information System (SIS), if it tracks user access
   - Copies of signed consent forms under Section (b)(2)(A)
   - Records of subpoena compliance under Section (b)(2)(B)
   - Records of transfers to other school systems under Section (b)(1)(B)
   - Any disclosures to outside agencies, researchers, or organizations under the various (b)(1) exceptions

3. **Respond to the parent honestly within 45 days**, providing whatever information you can reconstruct and acknowledging gaps transparently.

### Systemic Remediation

4. **Implement a compliant logging system immediately.** Every disclosure outside of the (1)(A) school-officials exception must be recorded going forward, including the identity of the requester and their specific legitimate interest.

5. **Audit your SIS and other record systems** to determine whether any electronic audit trails exist that could serve as retrospective documentation.

6. **Update your district's FERPA policies and annual notifications** under **Section (e)** to reflect corrected practices.

7. **Consider consulting with your district's legal counsel** about whether to self-report the compliance gap, particularly if the parent files a complaint with the Department of Education's **Student Privacy Policy Office** (established under **Section (g)**).

---

## The Bottom Line

The statute is clear: the logging obligation is mandatory, the parent's right to see the log is explicit, and your district's failure to maintain it is a compliance failure. Your best path forward is **transparency, good-faith reconstruction, and immediate systemic correction** — not defensiveness or denial. A forthcoming posture significantly reduces the risk that this escalates to a formal complaint and federal enforcement action.
