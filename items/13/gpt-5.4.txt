Short answer: **possibly, but not as you describe unless the recipient qualifies and the data are properly protected under FERPA’s audit/evaluation rules**.

## Core FERPA rule
Under FERPA, personally identifiable information from education records generally cannot be redisclosed without consent unless an exception applies. As an SEA, your original collection likely relied on the **audit/evaluation exception** in **20 U.S.C. § 1232g(b)(1)(C)** and **(b)(3)**.

That exception allows disclosure to:
- **authorized representatives** of the SEA,
- for audit/evaluation/enforcement of federally or state-supported education programs,
- subject to strict limits, including protection against personal identification and destruction when no longer needed.

## Key issue: are the retained “unique student identifiers” still PII?
Very likely **yes**. Under FERPA, removing names and SSNs alone does **not** necessarily de-identify the data. If the third party receives persistent unique IDs that permit student-level longitudinal tracking, those IDs are typically treated as **personally identifiable information**, especially if they can reasonably be used to identify a student, directly or indirectly.

So this is **not automatically safe just because direct identifiers are removed**.

## When sharing may be permissible
The disclosure can be permissible **if** the third-party research organization is properly designated as your **authorized representative** for the audit/evaluation activity, and the disclosure satisfies FERPA’s audit/evaluation conditions.

That generally means:

1. **Purpose limitation**  
   The organization must use the data **only** to carry out the SEA’s audit/evaluation/enforcement activity for the specified education program.

2. **Authorized representative designation**  
   The organization must be formally acting **on behalf of** the SEA, not for its own independent research purposes.

3. **Written agreement / controls**  
   You should have a written agreement specifying:
   - the specific purpose of the disclosure,
   - the scope of the information disclosed,
   - that the recipient may use the data only for that FERPA-permitted purpose,
   - redisclosure restrictions,
   - data security requirements,
   - destruction or return of data when no longer needed.

4. **Protection against personal identification**  
   Under **§ 1232g(b)(3)**, the data must be protected so that personal identification of students/parents is not permitted except by authorized officials/representatives with a legitimate need for the audit/evaluation.

5. **Destruction when no longer needed**  
   Personally identifiable data must be destroyed when no longer needed for the audit/evaluation/enforcement purpose.

## What a contract “not to re-identify” does and does not do
A no-reidentification promise is **helpful but not sufficient by itself**. FERPA requires more than a simple contractual promise:
- the recipient must fit within a valid FERPA disclosure pathway,
- the SEA must maintain control consistent with the exception,
- the disclosure must be necessary for the audit/evaluation purpose,
- and the data must remain protected as PII if the unique identifiers allow linkage over time.

## If the third party is just an outside researcher
If the organization is receiving the dataset for its **own research study**, rather than as your authorized representative carrying out your evaluation, then the audit/evaluation exception may **not** cover the disclosure.

There is also FERPA’s **studies exception** in **§ 1232g(b)(1)(F)**, but that exception usually applies to disclosures by the educational agency/institution for certain study purposes and comes with its own conditions. Since you are an SEA holding data under the audit/evaluation exception, you should be cautious about assuming you can simply switch to another basis for redisclosure.

## Practical conclusion
**Yes, disclosure may be permissible under FERPA, but only if the third-party organization is your properly designated authorized representative for the audit/evaluation and the arrangement satisfies FERPA’s audit/evaluation requirements.**  

**No, it is not sufficient merely to strip names/SSNs while retaining unique student identifiers and rely on a contractual promise not to re-identify.** If the identifiers permit longitudinal linkage, the dataset likely still contains FERPA-protected PII.

## Recommended approach
Before disclosing, ensure:
- the third party is formally designated as an **authorized representative** of the SEA;
- the activity is squarely within the SEA’s **audit/evaluation** of the federally funded program;
- a detailed written agreement governs use, security, redisclosure, and destruction;
- only the **minimum necessary** data are shared;
- the unique identifiers are necessary and appropriately controlled;
- the universities’ disclosure to the SEA and the SEA’s onward disclosure are both documented consistently with FERPA.
