Based on the provided text of the Family Educational Rights and Privacy Act (FERPA), here is a legal analysis of your proposed data-sharing arrangement.

**The short answer:** Under the strict statutory language of FERPA, sharing this dataset is **only permissible if you officially designate the third-party research organization as your "authorized representative"** to conduct the audit and evaluation, or if the arrangement specifically qualifies under the "studies exception." Merely removing names and Social Security numbers while retaining unique student identifiers does not exempt the data from FERPA's restrictions.

Here is the detailed breakdown based on the statutory text:

### 1. The Audit and Evaluation Restriction (Subsection b)(3)
You collected this data under the audit and evaluation exception. Under **Subsection (b)(3)**, State educational authorities have access to student records necessary for the audit and evaluation of Federally-supported education programs. 

However, this access comes with a strict condition regarding re-disclosure: 
> *"any data collected by such officials shall be protected in a manner which will not permit the personal identification of students and their parents **by other than those officials**..."*

Because you are retaining "unique student identifiers," the dataset is still considered personally identifiable information (PII). Stripping direct identifiers like names and SSNs does not fully de-identify the data if a unique identifier allows for potential tracking or re-identification. Therefore, simply handing this PII over to an outside third party violates the provision that limits identification strictly to "those officials."

### 2. How You Can Legally Share the Data
To share this data without obtaining prior written consent from parents or eligible students, your arrangement must fit within one of the following specific statutory exceptions:

**Option A: Designate the Third Party as an "Authorized Representative" (Subsections b)(1)(C) and b)(3)**
Under **Subsection (b)(1)(C)**, funds are not withheld if records are released to *"authorized representatives of... State educational authorities, under the conditions set forth in paragraph (3)."* 
*   To do this, you must formally designate the research organization as an authorized representative of your SEA for the specific purpose of auditing or evaluating the Federally-supported program. 
*   They would be bound by the same restrictions as you: they cannot permit personal identification by anyone else, and the data **must be destroyed** when no longer needed for the audit/evaluation.

**Option B: The "Studies Exception" (Subsection b)(1)(F)**
If the statistical modeling is specifically being done for the purpose of *"improving instruction,"* you could potentially release the data to the organization under **Subsection (b)(1)(F)**.
*   This exception allows sharing with *"organizations conducting studies for, or on behalf of, educational agencies or institutions..."*
*   The condition here is identical: the study must not permit the personal identification of students by anyone other than the representatives of the organization, and the information **must be destroyed** when no longer needed for the purpose of the study.

### 3. Requirements for the Third-Party Transfer (Subsection b)(4)(B))
If you proceed under one of the legal avenues above, **Subsection (b)(4)(B)** imposes strict conditions on transferring personal information to a third party. 
*   The transfer must be on the condition that the third party *"will not permit any other party to have access to such information without the written consent of the parents of the student."*
*   You noted that the third party has contractually agreed not to attempt re-identification. You must also ensure the contract explicitly prohibits them from re-disclosing the data to any other party and mandates the destruction of the data when the longitudinal analysis/evaluation is complete. 
*   If the third party violates these terms (e.g., fails to destroy the information), FERPA dictates that your SEA *"shall be prohibited from permitting access to information from education records to that third party for a period of not less than five years."*

### Summary Recommendation
To remain compliant with FERPA, you cannot simply share this dataset as an independent transaction just because direct identifiers were removed. You must formally document that the third party is acting as an **authorized representative** of the SEA for the specific purpose of the program evaluation (or conducting a qualifying study on your behalf). Your contract must also explicitly mandate the protection against re-disclosure and the ultimate destruction of the unique identifiers when the evaluation is complete.
