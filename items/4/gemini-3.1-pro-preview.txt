**The Short Answer:**
Generally, **yes**. If these are minor children attending a K-12 school, denying their parents the right to inspect and review their education records is a direct violation of FERPA and puts your federal funding at risk. 

However, there is a major exception depending on the age and educational level of the students involved.

Here is the breakdown of the law based on the text provided:

### 1. The General Rule: Parents Have an Absolute Right to Access
Under **Subsection (a)(1)(A)**, no federal funds shall be made available to any educational agency that "has a policy of denying, or which effectively prevents, the parents of students... the right to inspect and review the education records of their children." 
*   **Timeframe:** You cannot deny them, nor can you delay indefinitely. You must establish procedures to grant this access "within a reasonable period of time, but in no case more than forty-five days after the request has been made."

### 2. The Crucial Exception: Transfer of Rights (Age 18 or College)
Before you grant access, you must determine the age and educational status of the students. Under **Subsection (d)**, the rights accorded to parents transfer *exclusively* to the student if the student:
1.  Has attained eighteen (18) years of age, **OR**
2.  Is attending an institution of postsecondary education.

If the students meet either of these criteria, you **must deny** the parents' request to access the records, because releasing them without the adult/postsecondary student's written consent would violate the student's privacy rights under **Subsection (b)(1)**.

*Exception to the Exception:* Under **Subsection (b)(1)(H)**, you *may* release the records to the parents of an 18+ or postsecondary student without the student's consent **if** the student is claimed as a dependent for tax purposes under Section 152 of Title 26.

### 3. Redacting Information About Other Students
If you grant the parents access, you must be careful about records that contain information about multiple students. Under **Subsection (a)(1)(A)**, if a material or document includes information on more than one student, the parent only has the right to inspect and review the specific parts of the document that relate to *their own child*. You must redact or withhold information regarding other students.

### Next Steps for the Superintendent:
1. **Verify the age and grade level of the students:** If they are under 18 and in K-12, you must grant the parents access. 
2. **Acknowledge the Request:** Inform the parents that the school has received their request and will provide access to the records within the legally mandated 45-day window.
3. **Review and Redact:** Ensure that the records being provided do not contain personally identifiable information about other students. 
4. **Do not charge for access:** While not explicitly detailed in this specific excerpt, standard FERPA practice allows you to charge for *copies* (unless doing so prevents access), but you cannot charge a fee for the parents to simply *inspect and review* the records.
