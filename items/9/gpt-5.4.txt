Yes, possibly — but only if the disclosure fits FERPA’s **studies exception** and you structure it correctly.

### Short answer
Under **20 U.S.C. § 1232g(b)(1)(F)**, a district may disclose education records **without parental consent** to:

> “organizations conducting studies for, or on behalf of, educational agencies or institutions for the purpose of developing, validating, or administering predictive tests, administering student aid programs, and improving instruction,”

**if**:
1. the study is **for or on behalf of** the district or school,
2. it is for one of the permitted FERPA purposes, most relevant here: **improving instruction**,
3. the study is conducted so that the researchers do **not permit personal identification** of students/parents except by their own authorized representatives, and
4. the data are **destroyed when no longer needed** for the study.

### Key issue for your scenario
A university lab wants student-level data to study correlations between socioeconomic factors and academic outcomes, with publication in a journal.

That is **not automatically allowed** just because it is academic research.

The central FERPA question is:

## Is the university conducting the study **for or on behalf of the district**, and is the study for a permitted purpose such as **improving instruction**?

If the answer is **yes**, then disclosure without consent may be permissible.

If the answer is **no** — for example, if the university is doing a general scholarly project primarily for its own research/publication purposes — then **FERPA would not allow disclosure without consent** under the studies exception.

---

## What must be true for disclosure without consent

### 1. The study must be for or on behalf of the district
The district should be able to say the university is acting as its agent/contractor to conduct a study the district is using for an educational purpose.

Bad facts:
- university independently proposes a paper topic,
- district has no operational use for the results,
- agreement says data are for the university’s research agenda.

Better facts:
- district commissions or sponsors the study,
- study is tied to district decision-making,
- agreement says the study is being conducted **for the district** to evaluate or improve educational practices.

### 2. The purpose must fit FERPA
The most plausible permitted purpose here is **improving instruction**.

A broad study of socioeconomic factors and outcomes may qualify **if** the district can reasonably connect it to instructional improvement, intervention design, resource allocation, or support strategies.

If it is purely descriptive social science research with no real instructional nexus, the fit is weaker.

### 3. The district needs a written agreement
While your quoted statute states the basic rule, FERPA practice requires a formal agreement that limits use and disclosure. The agreement should clearly cover:
- purpose of the study,
- that it is conducted **for or on behalf of** the district,
- scope of data,
- prohibition on redisclosure,
- confidentiality/security safeguards,
- destruction timeline,
- publication only in aggregate/de-identified form.

### 4. Publication cannot identify students or parents
The statute requires the study to be conducted so it “will not permit the personal identification of students and their parents” by anyone other than the organization’s representatives.

So:
- published results should be aggregated or de-identified,
- small-cell suppression and disclosure avoidance should be used,
- no student-level identifiable data in publications.

### 5. Data must be destroyed when no longer needed
The university cannot keep identifiable data indefinitely for future unspecified research.

---

## Practical risk point
If the university wants the data mainly because:
- it wants a publishable dataset,
- it will use the records for its own academic research,
- it may reuse the data later,

then I would be cautious. That often looks less like a FERPA-permitted “study for or on behalf of” the district and more like an independent research project, which generally would require:
- **parental consent**, or
- truly **de-identified data** outside FERPA.

---

## Safer alternatives

### Option 1: Share only de-identified data
If the district can properly de-identify the data so students are not reasonably identifiable, FERPA does not restrict the disclosure as education records with PII.

This is often the cleanest path for publication-oriented research.

### Option 2: Structure it as a FERPA studies-exception agreement
If the district genuinely wants the study for district purposes, use a strong written agreement and disclose only the minimum necessary data.

### Option 3: Obtain consent
If the study does not comfortably fit the studies exception, parental consent is the most conservative route.

---

## Bottom line
**Yes, you may be able to share student-level records without parental consent under FERPA, but only if the university is conducting the study for or on behalf of the district for a FERPA-permitted purpose such as improving instruction, with strict limits on identification, redisclosure, and retention.**

**No, you should not rely on FERPA to share identifiable student records without consent if this is merely the university’s independent research project for publication.**
